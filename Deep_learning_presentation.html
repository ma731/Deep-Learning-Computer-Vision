Spoilage Forecasting

SPEAKER: Jorge. Hand-off from Bassem.

LSTM Design

A model that first lost, then learned

First run: a count-only LSTM lost to the one-lag naive baseline by 6.8% MAE.
Root cause: the naive baseline copies last week's matching weekday. That is enough for regular seasonality. What it cannot do is anticipate a multi-day holiday surge or an end-of-month markdown block, because those effects are keyed to the calendar, not to past counts.

The fix: 6 calendar features per day added to the window. Day-of-week and day-of-year sin/cos encodings, a national-holiday flag, and a Law 1/2025 promotion flag. Input shape: (28, 7) instead of (28, 1). The LSTM can now anticipate shocks the naive lag structurally cannot see.

SPEAKER: Jorge. I want to show you the moment our model failed, and how we fixed it. That arc is where the real technical depth is. Version one was a univariate LSTM reading only past counts. It lost to a trivial one-lag naive baseline by 6.8 percent MAE. That happened because the naive baseline copies last week's same weekday. On a regular weekly-seasonal series, that is already hard to beat with a shallow model. The LSTM had no way to know that a three-day holiday weekend or an end-of-month markdown block was coming. So we added six calendar features. They are leakage-free because dates are known in advance. Same principle as exogenous regressors in SARIMAX or Prophet. That change swung performance by roughly 49 percentage points.

SPEAKER: Jorge. After the calendar-feature fix, the LSTM beats the naive baseline by 42.7 percent on MAE and 43.8 percent on RMSE. MAE of 3.59. Noise floor is approximately 3.19, the irreducible error you get when you predict the deterministic signal perfectly and only the daily noise remains. We are within 12 percent of that floor. That is near-optimal performance. Training stopped at epoch 51, best weights around epoch 42, and validation loss stayed below training loss throughout. No overfitting. Split is strictly chronological. Test set was never touched during model selection.

Forecasting Detail

Why the noise floor matters

The simulation injects N(0, 4) daily noise. A one-lag naive forecast carries two independent noise draws, so its theoretical lower bound on MAE is approximately 4.5, even before calendar shocks add additional error on shock days.

A predictor that nails the deterministic signal perfectly achieves MAE of approximately 3.19, one noise draw, unavoidable. Our LSTM reaches 3.59. The gap of 0.40 is noise, not model weakness.

The right question is not "did you beat the baseline?" but "how far are you from the ceiling?" We are 12% from the ceiling.

Backup slide. Noise floor = 4 * sqrt(2/pi) = approximately 3.19. Our MAE of 3.59 minus 3.19 = 0.40 unexplained. That is less than one item per day. Acceptable for store planning.

Forecasting Detail

Why calendar features are valid

  • Calendar shocks are real: Spanish national public holidays and Law 1/2025 end-of-month markdown blocks.
  • Calendar features are leakage-free: dates are known in advance. Standard practice in SARIMAX, Prophet, and any production forecasting system.
  • The model lands within 12% of the irreducible noise floor. It is not beating a strawman. It is near-optimal.
Backup slide. Lead with the third point: the noise-floor framing is the hardest to dismiss. Calendar features are genuinely known in advance, not post-hoc data leakage.
