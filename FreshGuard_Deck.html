The problem

Supermarkets bin produce they could have sold.

4–6%
of produce revenue lost to shrink, every year

Decay is caught too late: a busy employee eyeballs the shelf once a day. By the time a tomato "looks bad enough", it's bin material. One day earlier it could have been marked down and still sold.

Spain's Law 1/2025 now requires retailers to discount or donate produce nearing the end of its life, instead of discarding it.
The catch: loose produce has no printed expiry date. A single apple in a bin carries no label. Visual condition is the only available signal, and that is exactly what a CNN is good at.
SPEAKER — MARCO (00:50). Stores throw away four to six percent of the fruit and veg they buy, and most of it is avoidable, it's a timing problem. Decay gets caught a day too late, after the markdown window has closed. On top of that, Spain's Law 1/2025 makes acting on near-expiry food a legal requirement, donate or discount, not bin. But here's the gap that makes it an AI problem: a packaged yogurt has a date; a loose apple does not. For loose produce, how it *looks* is the only signal you have. That's the opening for computer vision.

The ANN flattens the image, which throws away all spatial structure — it tops out at 31.9 percent, and it's the *biggest* model at 14 million parameters. A CNN from scratch adds convolution, the right inductive bias for images, and jumps to 75 percent with fewer parameters. Then MobileNetV2, pretrained on ImageNet and fine-tuned, reaches 95.8 percent with just 2.6 million trainable parameters — the best accuracy *and* the smallest model. The lesson isn't "bigger is better." It's that the right architecture beats raw capacity. Let me show you how the transfer model gets there.
The core model · MobileNetV2

Borrow the eyes. Teach the judgment.

MobileNetV2 already learned edges, textures and colour from 1.4M ImageNet images. We keep that, attach a small head, and specialise in two phases.

Result: 95.8% test accuracy with only 2.6M trainable parameters. That is the transfer-learning payoff.

SPEAKER — YAXIN (03:55). Transfer learning works because the hard part — learning what edges and textures look like — is already done. Phase one: we freeze the backbone and train only a new head at a normal learning rate, so we can't damage the pretrained weights. Phase two: we unfreeze just the top 30 layers and fine-tune at a learning rate a hundred times smaller, one-e-minus-five, so we specialise to fruit-rot without erasing what ImageNet taught it. One design detail that matters later: we build the model flat, un-nested, so Grad-CAM can address the last convolutional layer directly. Bassem will show you that the model is not just accurate, it's trustworthy.

IV

Spoilage forecasting

The honest story: our first model lost to a trivial baseline. How we fixed it is the real depth.

Now presentingJorge Vildoso
SPEAKER — JORGE (05:25). Thanks Bassem. I built the forecasting model and the manager dashboard. I want to show you a failure first, because fixing it is where the technical depth is.
LSTM design · the negative result

A model that first lost, then learned.

First run. A count-only LSTM lost to the one-lag naive baseline by 6.8% MAE. Copying "last week's same weekday" is hard to beat on a seasonal series.
Root cause. The naive baseline can't anticipate a multi-day holiday surge or an end-of-month markdown block. Those shocks are keyed to the calendar, not to past counts.
The fix. Add 6 calendar features per day (day-of-week & day-of-year as sin/cos, a holiday flag, a Law 1/2025 promo flag). Input goes from (28, 1) to (28, 7), and the LSTM can finally see the shocks coming. Leakage-free: dates are known in advance.
SPEAKER — JORGE (05:40). Version one was a univariate LSTM reading only past counts, and it lost to a trivial one-lag naive baseline by almost seven percent. That's not embarrassing, it's diagnostic: the naive baseline copies last week's matching weekday, which is genuinely strong on a weekly-seasonal series. What it *cannot* do is know a three-day holiday weekend or an end-of-month markdown block is coming, because those are tied to the calendar, not to recent counts. So we gave the LSTM six calendar features — cyclical day encodings, a holiday flag, a promotion flag. Input shape goes from 28-by-1 to 28-by-7. And these are leakage-free, because dates are known in advance, the same way exogenous regressors work in SARIMAX or Prophet. Here's what that bought us.

Appendix · forecasting detail

Why the noise floor is the honest yardstick.

The simulation injects N(0, 4) daily noise. A one-lag naive forecast carries two independent noise draws, a theoretical MAE lower bound near 4.5, before any calendar shock adds error.

A predictor that nails the deterministic signal perfectly carries one draw: MAE ≈ 3.19 = 4 · √(2/π), unavoidable. Our LSTM reaches 3.59. The 0.40 gap is noise, less than one item a day.

12% from the ceiling is a far stronger claim than "beat the baseline." It bounds how much performance is even left to win.
Backup. Noise floor = 4·√(2/π) ≈ 3.19. Our 3.59 minus 3.19 = 0.40 unexplained — acceptable for store-level planning.
Appendix · forecasting detail

Why calendar features aren't leakage.

Real shocks. Spanish national holidays (stores close, rotation stalls) and end-of-month Law 1/2025 markdown blocks. These are causal, not coincidental.
Leakage-free. Dates are known in advance. This is exactly how exogenous regressors work in SARIMAX and Prophet, standard production practice.
Near-optimal, not a strawman. The model lands within 12% of the irreducible noise floor. The lift is real signal, not an inflated baseline.
Backup. Lead with the noise-floor point — it's the hardest to dismiss. Calendar features are genuinely known ahead of time, so there's no future leaking into training.
Appendix · robustness without retraining

Five inference-time tricks for the real floor.

Gray-world white balance
Neutralise warm store lighting so an apple stops drifting toward "orange." (classic CV)
Test-time augmentation
Average the crop and its mirror, steadier on hard / small produce.
Saliency crop
Lock onto the most saturated blob so it sees the strawberry, not the table.
Temporal majority vote
15-frame softmax history per tracked item, so one odd frame can't flip the verdict.
Decay severity via HSV (classic CV, not the net) measures the browned/bruised surface fraction, which drives whether "sell soon" means −20% or −40%.
Backup. A model that scores 95.8% on clean images can still wobble on a shaky phone under tungsten light. Rather than retrain, we added five inference-time tricks. The last one, HSV decay severity, is how a softmax becomes a specific markdown percentage.
Appendix · honesty & stack

What we'd fix, and what it's built on.

Honest limitations

· Domain shift: trained on clean Kaggle images; real stores need a small calibration set (the review queue is built for it).

· Forecast history is simulated with Spanish seasonality; the method is real, live scans feed in.

· One laptop CPU can't grade a fast belt: production batches frames or adds an edge GPU.

· YOLO boxes only 4 of 10 types (COCO); the other 6 use a center-crop fallback.

· € projections are illustrative, not measured savings.

Tech stack

Deep learning · TensorFlow / Keras (CNN + LSTM), Ultralytics YOLOv8 + ByteTrack

Classic CV · OpenCV (white balance, HSV mask, saliency)

ML tooling · scikit-learn (metrics, t-SNE, scaler), pandas, numpy

Backend · FastAPI + Uvicorn, SQLite scan log

Frontend · vanilla HTML/CSS/JS, anime.js, qrcode.js (no framework)

Backup. A good project knows its own weak spots. The biggest real-world risk is domain shift; the active-learning queue is our answer. Stack is deliberately lightweight — it runs on a laptop and a phone.
